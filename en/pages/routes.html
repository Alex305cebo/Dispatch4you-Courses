🛤️ Main Interstate Corridors

0:00 / 0:00
Track 1 of 5

The Interstate Highway System is the circulatory system of American freight. Four major corridors connect the country east to west and north to south. A dispatcher must know each of them: which cities they link, what freight moves along them, where the traffic jams are, where the tolls are, and which seasonal problems to expect. Understanding the corridors means understanding how the money moves in trucking.

I-95

East Coast Corridor

Maine → Miami, FL (1,920 miles)

The busiest Interstate on the East Coast. It connects every major city on the eastern seaboard: Boston, New York, Philadelphia, Baltimore, Washington DC, Richmond, Jacksonville, Miami. It is the artery for consumer goods, e-commerce, and produce out of Florida.

Challenges: chronic congestion around NYC and DC, high tolls (NJ Turnpike — up to $100+ for trucks), narrow stretches in Connecticut and Rhode Island. In winter — snowstorms from Virginia to Maine. Despite the difficulties, I-95 is one of the most profitable corridors thanks to the density of population and business.

I-10

Southern Corridor

Jacksonville, FL → Los Angeles, CA (2,460 miles)

The main southern corridor from the Atlantic to the Pacific. It runs through Jacksonville, Tallahassee, Mobile, New Orleans, Houston, San Antonio, El Paso, Tucson, Phoenix, and Los Angeles. Critically important for cross-border freight (Laredo, El Paso) and the oil and gas industry (Houston).

Notable features: West Texas — 550 miles of nearly empty space between San Antonio and El Paso (a classic deadhead stretch). Hurricane season (June–November) can shut down sections along the Gulf Coast. In summer, temperatures hit 110°F+ in Arizona — reefer equipment runs at its limit.

I-80

Northern Corridor

Teaneck, NJ → San Francisco, CA (2,900 miles)

The main northern transcontinental corridor. It runs through New Jersey, Pennsylvania, Ohio, Indiana, Illinois (Chicago), Iowa, Nebraska, Wyoming, Utah, Nevada, and California (San Francisco). This is the primary route for coast-to-coast freight through the Midwest.

Critical stretches: Wyoming (Elk Mountain, 8,640 ft) — the strongest winds and snowstorms in winter, the road closes several times a season. Donner Pass in the Sierra Nevada (CA/NV border) — chain requirements in winter. Ohio Turnpike — tolls $30+ for trucks. Despite the winter difficulties, I-80 is a key corridor for intermodal and dry van freight.

🌎 US Regions for a Dispatcher

0:00 / 0:00
Track 2 of 5

The US breaks down into 5 key regions, each with its own freight characteristics. A dispatcher needs to understand which loads dominate each region, what rates to expect, and which seasonal swings to account for. Click a region card to see the details.

🏙️

Northeast

NY, NJ, PA, MA, CT, ME, NH, VT, RI

The most densely populated region. A huge volume of consumer goods, but tricky logistics due to narrow roads, congestion, and truck restrictions in the cities.

More ▼

The Northeast is the economic center of the US, with a population of over 55 million. New York, Boston, Philadelphia, and other metro areas create constant demand for goods delivery. But working here takes experience: narrow streets, delivery windows, tolls, and difficult parking.

  • Average RPM: $2.50–$3.50 (above average because of the difficulty)
  • Main freight: consumer goods, electronics, pharmaceuticals
  • Ports: Newark, Boston — import freight off container ships
  • In winter: snowstorms, delays, higher rates
  • Tolls: NJ Turnpike, George Washington Bridge — $15–$100+ for trucks
  • Restrictions: many NYC streets are off-limits to 53' trailers
🌴

Southeast

FL, GA, SC, NC, VA, TN, AL, MS, LA

A fast-growing region with strong logistics infrastructure. Atlanta is one of the country's largest transport hubs. Florida is a key produce market.

More ▼

The Southeast is experiencing a boom in population and business growth. Companies are relocating warehouses and distribution centers here from the expensive Northeast. Atlanta has become the second most important logistics hub after Chicago. The Port of Savannah is one of the fastest-growing in the country.

  • Average RPM: $2.00–$2.80
  • Atlanta: junction of I-75, I-85, I-20 — freight in every direction
  • Florida produce season: January–April, rates out of FL spike
  • Port of Savannah, GA: container freight, growing 15% a year
  • Hurricane season (June–November): emergency freight, high rates
  • Charlotte, Nashville, Raleigh — fast-growing markets
🌾

Midwest

IL, OH, MI, IN, WI, MN, MO, IA, KS, NE

The heart of American industry and agriculture. Chicago is the country's largest transport hub. Steady demand year-round.

More ▼

The Midwest is the "Crossroads of America." More freight passes through Chicago than through any other city. The region produces automobiles (Detroit), farm products (Iowa, Kansas), and industrial goods (Ohio, Indiana). For a dispatcher, it's one of the most predictable regions in terms of volume and rates.

  • Average RPM: $1.80–$2.50
  • Chicago: I-90, I-94, I-80, I-55 — access to the whole country
  • Harvest season (September–November): grain, corn, soybeans — flatbed and hopper
  • Detroit: auto parts, just-in-time delivery for the plants
  • Winter: heavy snowstorms, blizzards, delays on I-80 and I-90
  • Columbus, OH: the largest warehousing hub (Amazon, Walmart)
🏜️

Southwest

TX, AZ, NM, OK, AR

Texas is the second-largest state and one of the biggest freight markets. Dallas, Houston, San Antonio — powerful hubs with an enormous volume of freight.

More ▼

Texas is an economy of its own, the size of a small country. Houston is the oil and gas capital, Dallas-Fort Worth is a logistics giant, Laredo is the largest border crossing with Mexico. For a dispatcher, Texas always means freight: oilfield equipment, construction materials, consumer goods, cross-border freight.

  • Average RPM: $1.80–$2.60
  • Dallas-Fort Worth: 3rd-largest freight hub in the country
  • Laredo, TX: 40% of all US-Mexico trade passes through here
  • Houston: oil & gas, chemicals, port — 2nd-largest by volume in the US
  • Summer heat: 110°F+ — reefer loads demand attention
  • Phoenix, AZ: fast-growing market, but a deadhead zone heading west
🌊

West Coast

CA, WA, OR, NV, CO, UT

California is the largest economy among the states. The ports of LA/Long Beach handle 40% of all US imports. Produce season here lasts almost the entire year.

More ▼

The West Coast is the gateway for Asian imports and the country's largest producer of farm products. California alone produces more than 50% of all US fruits and vegetables. The ports of Los Angeles and Long Beach are the start of the supply chain for millions of goods. But the region has its own challenges: strict environmental rules (CARB), mountain passes, and long distances.

  • Average RPM: $2.00–$3.00 (outbound), $1.50–$2.00 (inbound)
  • LA/Long Beach: 40% of US imports, container freight
  • Central Valley, CA: produce year-round — lettuce, strawberries, almonds
  • CARB compliance: only newer trucks (2010+) are allowed into CA
  • Seattle/Tacoma: port + Amazon HQ + Boeing — steady demand
  • Donner Pass, Grapevine: winter closures, chain requirements
⛰️

Mountain & Plains

MT, WY, ND, SD, ID, NE (west)

The least populated region. Long distances between cities, few loads for the return trip. A classic deadhead zone, but with opportunities in season.

More ▼

Mountain & Plains is a challenge for a dispatcher. The distances are enormous, there are few towns, and there is often no load for the return trip. But during harvest season (grain, sugar beets) rates can be very attractive. Oil regions (the Bakken in North Dakota) also create demand for equipment and materials.

  • Average RPM: $1.50–$2.20 (heavily season-dependent)
  • Deadhead: often 200+ miles to the next load
  • Harvest season: high rates for grain haulers and flatbed
  • Winter: extreme conditions, -30°F, road closures
  • I-90, I-94: the main corridors, but few alternatives
  • Tip: plan your exit from the region ahead of time, look for a backhaul before you enter

🕐 US Time Zones

The continental US has 4 time zones. This is critically important for a dispatcher: pickup and delivery times are given in local time. If you work from Eastern Time and your driver is on Pacific — that's a 3-hour difference. A time-zone mistake can cost you the load and your reputation.

🌅

Eastern (ET)

UTC-5 / UTC-4 (DST)

NY, FL, GA, OH, PA, VA, NC, SC, MI, IN (part). Most brokers and shippers operate on ET. Load boards refresh first on ET.

🏙️

Central (CT)

UTC-6 / UTC-5 (DST)

TX, IL, MN, WI, MO, AL, MS, LA, TN, AR, OK, KS, IA, NE. Chicago and Dallas are key hubs. Difference from ET: -1 hour.

⛰️

Mountain (MT)

UTC-7 / UTC-6 (DST)

CO, AZ, NM, UT, MT, WY, ID. Arizona does NOT observe daylight saving time (except the Navajo Nation). Difference from ET: -2 hours.

🌊

Pacific (PT)

UTC-8 / UTC-7 (DST)

CA, WA, OR, NV. The ports of LA/Long Beach operate on PT. When it's 8:00 AM in NY — it's only 5:00 AM in LA. Difference from ET: -3 hours.

📅 Seasonality & Weather

0:00 / 0:00
Track 5 of 5

Freight in the US depends heavily on the season. Produce season, holiday season, harvest — all of it affects rates, load availability, and road conditions. An experienced dispatcher plans routes with seasonal patterns in mind and earns more. Switch the tabs to see the details for each season.

🌱 Spring (March — May)

Spring is the start of produce season. Florida and California begin large-scale shipments of fruits and vegetables to the north and east. Reefer truck rates rise by 20-40%. Construction season kicks off in the northern states — demand for flatbed increases. This is one of the best periods to earn.

  • Florida produce: tomatoes, strawberries, citrus → Northeast
  • California produce: lettuce, strawberries → Midwest, East Coast
  • Construction materials: lumber, steel → northern states
  • Reefer RPM: $2.50–$3.50 (peak season)
  • Weather: tornadoes in Tornado Alley (TX, OK, KS) — delays
  • Tip: book reefer loads out of FL and CA ahead of time

☀️ Summer (June — August)

Peak produce season. California ships maximum volumes. Construction is in full swing across the country. But the heat creates problems: reefer equipment runs at its limit, drivers tire faster, and tires blow more often on the hot asphalt. Hurricane season starts in June — the Gulf Coast is at risk.

  • Central Valley, CA: peak shipments — almonds, grapes, peaches
  • Beverage season: water, drinks — demand for dry van rises
  • Heat 100°F+: reefer problems, tire blowouts on I-10
  • Hurricanes (June–November): emergency freight, rates x3–x5
  • Back-to-school (August): rising demand for consumer goods
  • Tip: watch the hurricane forecast, prep your drivers for emergency loads

🍂 Fall (September — November)

Fall is the most profitable period in freight. Harvest season in the Midwest (grain, corn, soybeans), holiday season (getting ready for Black Friday and Christmas), and produce out of CA continues. Rates hit their yearly peak in October-November. This is the time when a dispatcher can earn the most.

  • Harvest: grain, corn, soybeans from IA, IL, IN, OH → ports and elevators
  • Holiday freight: retailers fill warehouses for Black Friday
  • Amazon peak season: October–December, huge demand
  • Dry Van RPM: $2.50–$3.00+ (yearly peak)
  • First frosts: Midwest and Northeast, reefer settings change
  • Tip: maximize your loading in October-November, it's the peak of the year

❄️ Winter (December — February)

Winter is a tough period. Holiday season continues into December, but after New Year's comes the "January slump" — the slowest month of the year. Rates drop by 20-30%. Snowstorms and ice in the northern states cause delays and dangerous conditions. But Florida produce starts in January, and emergency freight from winter storms can be very profitable.

  • December: the last push of holiday freight, high rates
  • January: the "January slump" — rates at a minimum, few loads
  • Snow and ice: I-80, I-90, I-94 — frequent closures and delays
  • Florida produce: January–March, reefer rates out of FL rise
  • De-icing salt: emergency salt deliveries to the northern states
  • Tip: send drivers south (FL, TX) in January-February

🔄 Deadhead & Backhaul Strategies

Deadhead (empty miles) is the number-one enemy of profitability. Every mile without a load is fuel cost, wear, and the driver's time with no income. The average cost of a deadhead mile is $1.50–$2.00 (fuel + wear & tear). If a driver runs 200 miles empty — that's $300–$400 in losses. An experienced dispatcher minimizes deadhead through strategic backhaul (return load) planning. Here are proven strategies:

01 🔍

Look for a backhaul BEFORE booking

Before you take a load to a certain point, check whether there are return loads out of that region. Use DAT, Truckstop.com, or Convoy to search. If there are few loads out of the delivery point — either pass on the load, or build the deadhead into the rate of the current load.

Example: A load Chicago → Fargo, ND pays $3,500. But there are no return loads out of Fargo — 250 miles of deadhead to Minneapolis. Real RPM: $3,500 ÷ (850 + 250) = $3.18. Without accounting for deadhead it looked like $4.12/mi.
02 🔗

Build load chains (relay)

Instead of one long route, plan a chain of 2-3 short loads. This minimizes deadhead and increases the total RPM. Each next pickup should be within 50-100 miles of the previous delivery.

Example: Instead of Chicago → Miami ($2.20/mi), build: Chicago → Nashville ($2.60/mi) → Atlanta ($2.50/mi) → Miami ($2.40/mi). Higher total income, minimal deadhead.
03 📊

Know your headhaul and backhaul directions

Every region has directions with high demand (headhaul) and low demand (backhaul). Outbound from CA, FL, TX is usually headhaul with high rates. Inbound to these states is backhaul. Use DAT RateView to analyze rates by lane.

Rule: LA outbound → $2.80+/mi. LA inbound → $1.50/mi. If a driver is heading INTO California — take any load, even a cheap one. Coming out of California — negotiate for the maximum.
04 📅

Use seasonal patterns

Produce season (spring-summer) creates headhaul out of FL and CA. Holiday season (fall) — headhaul out of ports and distribution centers. Harvest (fall) — headhaul out of the Midwest. Plan your drivers' routes with seasonal freight flows in mind.

Example: January-March: send drivers to FL (produce season begins). April-September: CA Central Valley (peak produce). October-November: ports and Midwest (holiday + harvest).
05 🤝

Build relationships with brokers in key zones

In deadhead zones (Arizona, Montana, Maine), loads rarely show up on the boards. But local brokers have regular shippers. Build relationships with 2-3 brokers in each problem zone — they'll give you loads that aren't on DAT.

Tip: Find brokers who specialize in Phoenix, AZ and Boise, ID. When a driver delivers a load to these cities — you already have a contact for a backhaul. That turns a deadhead zone into a manageable situation.
06 💡

The "triangle" rule

If a direct backhaul isn't possible — build a triangular route. Instead of A→B→A (where B→A is deadhead), plan A→B→C→A, where every leg has a load. This takes more planning, but it significantly increases income.

Example: A driver is based in Chicago. Instead of Chicago → Phoenix → Chicago (deadhead 1,750 mi), build: Chicago → Phoenix ($2.40/mi) → LA ($2.80/mi, produce) → Dallas ($2.60/mi) → Chicago ($2.30/mi). Zero deadhead, maximum income.

⚠️ Deadhead Zones & Problem Regions

Deadhead is empty miles without a load. Some regions are known for being hard to find a return load out of. A dispatcher must know these zones and plan routes so as to minimize deadhead miles. Every empty mile is lost money.

🏜️ Southern Arizona / New Mexico

One of the worst regions for a return load. After delivering to Phoenix or Tucson, a driver is often forced to run 200-400 miles empty to the next load. Little industry, little population, few warehouses.

High risk

🌾 Rural Midwest (ND, SD, MT)

Outside of harvest season — practically a desert for freight. Distances between cities of 100-300 miles, few loads. In harvest season the situation changes, but the window is short (2-3 months).

High risk

🌲 Northern Maine / Vermont

The far northeast — little industry, little population. After delivering to Bangor or Burlington, a return load is hard to find. In winter the situation is even worse because of the weather.

Medium risk

🏔️ West Virginia / Eastern Kentucky

Mountainous terrain with limited infrastructure. Narrow roads, few large warehouses and distribution centers. The coal industry is shrinking — there is less freight.

Medium risk

🌵 West Texas (El Paso corridor)

Between El Paso and San Antonio — 550 miles with a minimum of freight. I-10 through West Texas is one of the longest deadhead stretches. Cross-border freight out of Juarez helps, but isn't always available.

High risk

🌿 Central Oregon / Eastern Washington

Outside of Portland and Seattle — few loads. Agricultural areas are active only in season. In winter, mountain passes can be closed, which further limits the options.

Medium risk

✅ Dispatcher's Route Checklist

Before you send a driver on a route, run through this checklist. Each item expands with a detailed explanation — click to see the details.

  • 📏 Check the distance and drive time ▼

    Use PC Miler (the industry standard) or Google Maps to calculate. Keep in mind that truck routing differs from car routing — not every road is open to trucks. Add 10-15% to the calculated time for unforeseen delays, fueling, and rest.

  • ⏰ Check the driver's HOS ▼

    Hours of Service: a driver can drive a maximum of 11 hours after 10 hours of rest. A 14-hour window from the start of the workday. A mandatory 30-minute break after 8 hours. A 70-hour limit over 8 days. Check the driver's ELD before booking a load.

  • 🕐 Account for time zones ▼

    Pickup and delivery times are always given in local time. If a load runs from Chicago (CT) to New York (ET) with delivery at 8:00 AM ET — the driver must plan on Eastern Time. A time-zone mistake = being late = a penalty or a lost load.

  • 🌤️ Check the weather along the route ▼

    In winter: check for snowstorms, ice, pass closures. In summer: tornadoes in Tornado Alley, hurricanes on the Gulf Coast, heat in the Southwest. Use weather.gov and 511 traffic info. Warn the driver ahead of time and have a plan B.

  • 💰 Calculate the RPM and profitability ▼

    RPM (Rate Per Mile) = total rate ÷ total miles (including deadhead). The minimum acceptable RPM depends on the region and equipment type, but is usually $2.00+. Account for tolls, fuel surcharge, and detention pay. Don't forget the deadhead miles to pickup.

  • 🔄 Find a return load (backhaul) ▼

    Before sending the driver, check for loads out of the delivery point back or in the direction you need. Use DAT, Truckstop, Convoy to search. If there are few loads out of the delivery region (a deadhead zone) — factor that into the rate of the current load. Better to take a bit cheaper but with a guaranteed backhaul.

  • ⚖️ Check weight and dimension restrictions ▼

    Maximum weight: 80,000 lbs (gross). But some states have bridge formula restrictions. Overweight permits are needed for loads over the limit. Oversize loads (wide, tall, long) require special permits and sometimes escort vehicles. Check the requirements of each state on the route.

  • 🛣️ Check toll roads and restrictions ▼

    Toll roads can cost $50-$200+ for a truck on a single route (NJ Turnpike, Ohio Turnpike, Illinois Tollway). Some shippers reimburse tolls, others don't. Confirm ahead of time. EZ-Pass / PrePass save time and money. Factor tolls into the route's profitability calculation.
